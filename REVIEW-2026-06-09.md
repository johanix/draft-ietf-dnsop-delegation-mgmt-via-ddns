# Review of draft-ietf-dnsop-delegation-mgmt-via-ddns-02

**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Reviewer:** Claude (fresh from-scratch critical read, three independent
review passes: logic/correctness, clarity/terminology, completeness/IETF-readiness)
**Version reviewed:** `-02.md` (1070 lines, working copy; datatracker
latest is `-01`).
**Method:** Independent of any prior STATUS/audit notes. Three reviewers
each took a distinct lens over the full text; findings below are the
deduplicated synthesis. Where multiple lenses independently flagged the
same issue it is noted ("flagged by all three" etc.).

Severity scale:
- **BLOCKER** — would be sent back at AD/IANA review; gates WG Last Call.
- **MAJOR** — a real spec-quality or correctness issue a DNSOP reviewer
  will notice.
- **MINOR / NIT** — polish, consistency, wording.

Headline: the draft is architecturally mature and well-motivated, but
**not yet LC-ready**. The dominant gaps are **IANA completeness**
(B1/B2) and **security rigor** (B4/M5/M6), plus a terminology cleanup
(M2) and a pass to convert essay-like front matter into spec prose
(M11). Roughly one focused revision (`-03`). Note: the old "75% / labeled-
method template" framing was about right on *amount* but wrong on the
*nature* of the work — the load-bearing items are IANA + security, not a
preconditions/threat-model template on the bootstrap methods.

## DISPOSITION (added 2026-06-09, after author review)

This section records the author's decisions and a second-pass
re-verification of the "logic" findings against operational reality.
**The findings below this section are the original review text, kept
verbatim for the record; where the disposition changes a finding's
status or severity, this section governs.**

### Author decisions on the design questions

- **B2 (EDE codes):** Register the three EDE codes in **this** draft's
  IANA Considerations. EDE = the always-available baseline error
  channel; the KeyState OPT (draft-berra-dnsop-keystate) = the richer,
  optional channel. The text will make that relationship explicit.
- **B4 (unsigned bootstrap):** **Lead with the RFC 8078 reuse** as the
  justification — the `unsigned` mechanism reuses RFC 8078's
  multiple-vantage-point acceptance method; its security properties and
  limitations are RFC 8078's, not newly introduced here (cite RFC 8078
  normatively, already done at §777). Frame: a compromised authoritative
  nameserver operator breaks the child regardless of this mechanism (a
  pre-existing exposure, out of scope); remedies (reputable providers;
  sign the zone → stronger paths) are out of scope. Scope the "as strong
  as DNSSEC" claims (§199, §636) to the cryptographic primitive + the
  signed (at-apex / at-ns) paths only; the `unsigned` path carries RFC
  8078's properties, not DNSSEC's. Fix the "arbitrarily difficult"
  overstatement (§702).
- **M7 / M7c (mutual auth):** Make response-signing **MUST when the
  child uses KeyState inquiries** (closes the forged-response attack of
  §511 where it bites), SHOULD generally. **Accept + document** that for
  an *unsigned parent* the child cannot securely bootstrap the
  receiver's key, so mutual auth degrades to manual / is unavailable by
  default; document the forged-response threat consequence. No new
  mechanism.
- **M8 (SVCB dependency):** **Define the SVCB-at-DSYNC-target mechanism
  in this draft** (self-contained). draft-berra-dnsop-announce-scanner
  becomes an informative "see also" / prior-art reference. Removes the
  publication-blocking normative dependency.

### Re-verification of the logic findings (second pass)

- **M1 (at-ns "both at apex AND") — NOT A BUG; reclassified to a short
  CLARITY note.** The mechanism is correct: for an unsigned child the
  owner publishes the KEY at the child apex so that the DNS providers
  can read it and **re-publish** it under
  `_sig0key.{child}._signal.{their-ns}` in their own (signed) zones.
  The apex KEY is the *source copy* the providers re-publish from. Fix =
  add a **very short** statement that the apex KEY is published so
  providers can re-publish it under the special name. Do **not** wander
  into zone-transfer-security (TSIG/XoT/etc.) — out of scope.
- **B4 (unsigned trust loop) — severity overstated; reduces to the
  wording/scoping fix in the B4 decision above.** The draft already
  states (§697–703) that the `unsigned` path "does not provide the
  provable authenticity of the two previous methods." The only real
  kernel is the "arbitrarily difficult" / "as strong as DNSSEC"
  overstatement — handled by the B4 decision.
- **M3 (BADKEY → bootstrap) — downgraded to a CLARITY note.** Author
  confirms **BADKEY means strictly "unknown"** ("Receiver has no access
  to this key"). The known-but-not-trusted / validation-failed states
  are signaled only via EDE, never via BADKEY. So BADKEY → bootstrap is
  correct; just state explicitly that BADKEY = unknown and that the
  other states arrive via EDE (so a child does not bootstrap when it
  should wait).
- **M9 (self-signed UPDATE semantics) — real, but CLARITY not logic.**
  The mechanism is sound (key marked only "known", validated before
  "trusted", §565–570). Add one sentence stating the self-signature
  proves *possession of the private key only*, not authority; authority
  comes from the subsequent validation. Cross-reference the
  MUST-NOT-delete-until-validated rule.
- **M4 (retry SHOULD-then-disclaim) — CONFIRMED real** (§401–408). Pick
  one: true SHOULD defaults (drop the "not normative" disclaimer) or
  lowercase guidance. Keep consistent with the same retry baseline used
  in keystate / zone-owner-intent.
- **M7 (response-signing SHOULD) — CONFIRMED real**, handled by the M7
  decision above.

**Lesson carried forward:** the logic reviewer's lens caught real issues
(M4, M7, M9) but also misjudged M1 and over-scoped B4/M3 by not
accounting for operational reality (provider re-publication; the
acknowledged-weak RFC 8078 path; BADKEY's single meaning). Treat
remaining "logic" claims as needing a reality check before editing, not
as automatically actionable.

## BLOCKER

### B1  The "bootstrap" SvcParamKey is never registered with IANA
**Location:** defined §746 ("SvcParamKey 'bootstrap'"), used throughout
§733–802; IANA Considerations §915–939.
The document defines a new SvcParamKey `bootstrap` with a value syntax
and four values (`at-apex`, `at-ns`, `unsigned`, `manual`) — a normative
protocol element. IANA Considerations registers only (a) the DSYNC
scheme value and (b) a `(KEY, _signal)` underscore-name entry. There is
no request to register the `bootstrap` SvcParamKey in the SVCB Parameter
Registry (RFC 9460 §14.3.2), and no registry/enumeration for the
mechanism token values. An AD/IANA reviewer will reject this.
**Fix:** add an IANA subsection registering the SvcParamKey, and a
registry (or enumerated values + registration policy) for the tokens.

### B2  New Extended DNS Error codes are described but never requested
**Location:** §"Communication in Case of Errors" §459–479; IANA §915.
The text reads as if this document defines a "set of new Extended DNS
Error Codes {{!RFC8914}}" for three states ("SIG(0) key is known, but
not yet trusted", "...known but validation failed", "automatic bootstrap
not supported; manual required"). RFC 8914 EDE codes are an IANA
registry. IANA Considerations omits them — internal inconsistency.
**Fix:** either register the three EDE codes here, or state explicitly
they are defined elsewhere (e.g. in the keystate draft) and adjust the
reference accordingly. Clarify the relationship among the three
overlapping signaling channels: EDE codes, the KeyState OPT
(berra-keystate), and the `bootstrap` SvcParamKey.

### B3  Scheme value is self-contradictory (TBD vs. "2")
**Location:** §270 ("Scheme=TBD"), §300 ("the number 2 in the wire
protocol"), §303–305, IANA table §923–925 ("TBD"). Flagged by all three
reviewers.
The core protocol element (new DSYNC scheme "UPDATE") is requested as
TBD, but the body hard-codes "2". RFC 9859 already assigns scheme 1
(NOTIFY); "2" is a guess that may collide.
**Fix:** use "TBD" consistently; remove the hard-coded "2"; let the IANA
table be the single source.

### B4  Unsigned-child bootstrap: trust loop does not close; security claim overstated
**Location:** "When Child Zone Is Unsigned" §664–703 (esp. §669–687);
related claims §199–201, §636–639, §697.
For an *unsigned* child the KEY RRset is served by the very nameservers
named in the current delegation — and the trust being bootstrapped is
control over that delegation (NS/DS). The "consistent from multiple
vantage points and multiple times → promote known→trusted" check is
therefore circular: anyone who controls (or is on-path to) the
authoritative servers can publish any KEY, get it trusted, and rewrite
the delegation, including the NS targets. Multi-vantage/multi-transport
consistency only defeats a transient on-path attacker. The RFC
8078/8071 analogy (§697) is materially weaker than claimed: CDS bootstrap
yields a DS that the child's own keys then chain-validate; here the
bootstrapped key authorizes *replacement of the nameservers themselves*.
The "as secure as DNSSEC" / "arbitrarily difficult" framing is
overstated.
**Fix:** state the bounded threat model explicitly — this method
authenticates "the current operator of the delegation," not "the
registrant," and cannot defend against a malicious/compromised current
nameserver operator. Recommend coupling with parent-side authorization
(registrar/EPP) for changes that alter NS targets. Scope the security
claims to the cryptographic primitive and the specific bootstrap path.

## MAJOR

### M1  "at-ns" requires a DNSSEC-signed apex KEY, contradicting its unsigned-child purpose
**Location:** SvcParamKey "at-ns" §762–775 vs. "When Child Nameserver Is
In A DNSSEC-signed Zone" §599–639.
§764 says at-ns works "by publishing the DNSSEC-signed KEY record both at
the zone apex AND at the special name." But §601 describes at-ns for the
case the child zone is *unsigned*, where an apex KEY cannot be
DNSSEC-signed. Direct contradiction an implementer hits immediately.
**Fix:** remove "both at the zone apex AND" — at-ns requires only the
`_sig0key.{child}._signal.{ns}` record in the signed nameserver zone.

### M2  "UPDATE Receiver" shifts across three senses (terminology)
**Location:** defined §137–139; usage throughout.
The definition ("the parent primary name server or a separate dedicated
service") already admits two senses; the body adds a third:
1. The parent's **primary name server** — §325–327.
2. A **separate dedicated parent-operated service** — §327–329,
   §910–913 (dominant sense).
3. The **registrar** — §689–695 ("the target ... is operated by the
   registrar (or ... forwarded to the registrar)"), §806 ("the parent
   (or registrar) DNS UPDATE Receiver"). A distinct commercial third
   party, contradicting "on behalf of the parent zone."
Naming also drifts: "UPDATE Receiver", "DNS UPDATE Receiver",
"the receiver"/"the recipient" (lowercase, also used for the generic
NOTIFY/UPDATE actor), "parent('s) UPDATE Receiver".
**Fix:** one canonical term ("UPDATE Receiver"), defined once as a
*logical role* that MAY be co-located with the primary, run as a separate
parent service, or operated by a third party (registrar) under the
parent's authority. Reword the definition's "on behalf of the parent
zone" to admit the registrar case. Reserve lowercase "receiver/recipient"
for the generic protocol actor in §166–213 only.

### M3  BADKEY → bootstrap logic is unsafe using only the normative RCODE layer
**Location:** "RCODE BADKEY" §375–381 vs. EDE states §459–479.
On BADKEY the child is told to fall back to bootstrap (§377). But the
draft separately defines "known, not yet trusted (wait)", "validation
failed (do not retry)", and "unknown (bootstrap)" (§468–471) — and the
disambiguating EDE/KeyState machinery is only *informative*. A
conformant child using only normative RCODEs cannot tell these apart and
is steered into wrong (re-)bootstrap in two of three cases.
**Fix:** specify default child behavior per EDE state; define what
BADKEY means without EDE (safe to re-initiate only because re-bootstrap
is idempotent — say so, and cross-reference the anti-deletion rule).

### M4  "No response" section asserts SHOULD defaults then disclaims them
**Location:** §383–408.
Says the response is "implementation dependent" (§389), gives SHOULD
numbers (wait ≥5 s, exponential backoff, ≤5 retries; §401–405), then
says "they are not normative wire-protocol requirements" (§408).
Using BCP 14 SHOULD and disclaiming it is self-contradictory.
**Fix:** pick one — true SHOULD defaults (drop the disclaimer) or
lowercase non-normative guidance (drop the keywords). Also review the
RCODE-interpretation prose (§360–381): behavior stated as lowercase
"should be interpreted as" may need BCP 14 keywords.

### M5  The core authorization rule is lowercase, not normative
**Location:** §337–343.
"it is strongly recommended that the policy is further tightened by only
allowing updates to the delegation information of a child zone with the
exact same name as the name of the SIG(0) key that signed the UPDATE
request." This is *the* parent-side authorization control, expressed as
lowercase "strongly recommended."
**Fix:** make it SHOULD (or MUST with stated exceptions); surface it
normatively in Security Considerations.

### M6  Security Considerations too thin for the threat surface
**Location:** §889–913.
A mechanism that lets a child rewrite its own delegation (NS/DS/glue)
via injected UPDATEs and bootstraps trust keys over DNS, but Security
Considerations cover only "do the same checks as a scanner" + DoS/
rate-limiting. Missing: bootstrap downgrade / unsigned-path substitution
(could an attacker controlling answers force selection of the weaker
"unsigned" method via the `bootstrap` SvcParamKey?); UPDATE injection /
authorization scoping (the §337 key-name rule belongs here, normatively);
the re-bootstrap deletion protection (§841, currently only in body); the
receiver-key bootstrap trust gap (manual/unsigned fallback); anti-replay
(beyond SIG(0) inception/expiration — not discussed).
**Fix:** consolidated threat analysis covering the above.

### M7  Mutual-authentication gaps
**Location:** "Mutual Authentication" §507–524; receiver-key bootstrap
§705–731.
(a) Response-signing is only SHOULD (§515), so the very attack the
subsection exists to close — forged response claiming "key unknown" →
unnecessary re-bootstrap — remains open by default. (b) No stated
binding of the UPDATE *response* to the *request* (anti-replay), and the
KeyState inquiry that motivates mutual auth is carried in an OPT defined
in an informative draft whose auth is undefined here. (c) The child
bootstrapping the *receiver's* key depends on the parent zone being
DNSSEC-signed; for an *unsigned* parent it silently falls to "manual"
(§728), disabling mutual auth for exactly the resource-constrained
parents the draft targets (§156).
**Fix:** make response-signing MUST when KeyState inquiries are used;
specify replay binding; acknowledge the unsigned-parent degradation and
its threat consequence.

### M8  Possible normative dependency on an unadopted individual draft
**Location:** §740, SVCB "bootstrap" mechanism §733–802.
The SVCB-at-DSYNC-target mechanism (normative here) is described as
"utilizes the same mechanism as used in
{{?I-D.berra-dnsop-announce-scanner}}." If that mechanism is *defined*
there, this document has a normative dependency on an unadopted
individual I-D — a publication blocker — and the reference must be
normative.
**Fix:** clarify whether this document defines the SVCB-at-target
mechanism independently (preferred) or depends on berra-announce-scanner;
reconcile the reference classification accordingly.

### M9  Self-signed UPDATE semantics never stated (proof-of-possession only)
**Location:** "Bootstrapping the Child's Key" §553–570.
"Self-signed" means signed by the very key being added, which the
receiver does not yet trust. §565 gates processing on the self-signature
validating ("where the self-signature validates"), but validating it
proves only possession of the private key, not authorization. This
foundational property is never stated, and the mismatch case (attacker
injects a different self-signed key) is left implicitly to the §841
anti-deletion rule without a connecting cross-reference.
**Fix:** state explicitly that the self-signature provides
proof-of-possession only; authority comes solely from the subsequent
DNSSEC/out-of-band validation. Cross-reference §841 from here.

### M10  SIG(0) is defined incorrectly as "an asymmetric signing algorithm"
**Location:** Terminology §132–134; contradicts §198–199, §419.
SIG(0) (RFC 2931) is a transaction-authentication mechanism that *uses*
an asymmetric signing algorithm; it is not itself an algorithm. The
draft says so correctly elsewhere (§419 "Choice of SIG(0) Signature
Algorithm" treats the algorithm as a pluggable choice). This is the
first definition in Terminology and it is wrong.
**Fix:** "SIG(0): A DNS transaction authentication mechanism (RFC 2931)
that uses asymmetric (public-key) cryptography, so the recipient needs
only the sender's public key to verify a signature."

### M11  Essay/musing framing undercuts the spec
**Location:** §85–214; specific spots below.
- "Is there a Use Case?" as a top-level heading (§146) — rhetorical;
  rename to "Applicability" / "Motivation and Applicability".
- "This is not a new idea." opening a section (§217), with bare-text
  citation of `andrews-dnsop-update-parent-zones` (not a resolving
  `{{?...}}` reference).
- "it acts as a glorified generalized NOTIFY" (§207) — colloquial.
- §166–213 "DNS NOTIFY versus DNS UPDATE" reads as a tutorial; compress
  and fold the essential distinction into the Introduction.
**Fix:** convert front matter from musing to spec prose; fix the bare
citation.

## MINOR / NIT

- **(KEY, _signal) IANA likely duplicates RFC 9615** (§624 says _signal
  "is the one registered by RFC 9615"; §927 then *re-requests* it).
  Reconcile — likely no IANA action needed, just a reference. Separately,
  the `_sig0key` label introduced by this document (§626) is *not* in the
  IANA table and may itself need registration (or an explicit "no
  registration needed" rationale).
- **Port 0 in examples** (§713, §798: `DSYNC ANY UPDATE 0 updater...`)
  vs. `5302` used elsewhere (§287); §303–305 hard-codes "port 5302" in a
  normative interpretation block that should use "{port}".
- **Broken / stale anchor** `{{automatic-bootstrap-when-child-nameserver-
  is-in-a-dnssec-signed-zone}}` (§937) — actual heading (§599) anchors as
  `when-child-nameserver-is-in-a-dnssec-signed-zone`; xref will fail.
- **Markdown-style cross-references** `[...](#...)` (§760, §775, §782)
  instead of kramdown `{{...}}` used elsewhere (§937); may not render as
  section refs.
- **"Agent" used but undefined** (§297 child-side, §577 parent-side);
  define once or qualify each use.
- **"chapter"** used for an I-D (§528, §961) — should be "section".
- **REFUSED-as-permanent** (§367–373) ignores RFC 2136's existing
  meaning and transient causes (policy/ACL/rate-limit); tension with the
  Security Considerations rate-limit advice (§906). Soften to "currently
  unwilling"; don't mandate permanent give-up.
- **"as strong as DNSSEC-signatures"** (§199) conflates algorithm
  strength with protocol/trust-model security; scope the claim.
- **Transient vs. permanent DNSSEC-validation failure** during bootstrap
  (§595–597, §632–634) not distinguished; tie to the two EDE states.
- **Re-bootstrap "DEL ANY KEY"** (§835) vs. MUST-NOT-delete-until-
  validated (§841): reconcile explicitly — bootstrap UPDATEs are not
  processed with standard RFC 2136 atomic semantics; the receiver MUST
  defer the DEL until the ADD'd key validates.
- **"Limitation of Scope"** (§307) wedged between "Locating the Target"
  and "The DNS UPDATE Receiver"; move to just after Applicability.
- **KEY RRset listed as delegation data** (§318) — it is the bootstrap/
  auth channel, not delegation information; separate it.
- **"self-signed DNS UPDATE"** (§555, §832) used as a term of art without
  a Terminology entry.
- **Latin-abbreviation casing** inconsistent ("Eg." §391, "eg." §493,
  "I.e." §196/§300); normalize to "e.g."/"i.e.".
- **`date: {DATE}`** front matter placeholder (§5) — confirm it renders.
- **"TO BE REMOVED" GitHub note** (§77–81) and **Change History** (§957)
  — standard cruft, correctly marked; confirm the toolchain strips them.
- **No "assymmetric" typo** — the old STATUS item; correctly spelled
  "asymmetric" throughout. Already resolved.
